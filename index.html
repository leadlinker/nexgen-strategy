The Problems

Now that we know what is there, here is what those findings reveal about what is limiting your visibility with the 150 to 250 buyers who matter most.

Problem 1

Your Website Does Not Match the Caliber of Your Work

NexGen has national laboratory partnerships with Idaho National Labs, NETL, and Oak Ridge. You have a novel zero-emission graphite production process. You are building domestic LFP cathode manufacturing capacity. These are serious, credentialed projects. But when a DOE program manager, a GM procurement VP, or a battery factory supply chain director looks you up, they land on a GoDaddy template with stock photos and a generic contact form.

Here is what the buyer journey looks like today:

1

Referral or Introduction

Someone at Idaho National Lab, a DOE program office, or a trade conference mentions NexGen Materials as a domestic sourcing option.

2

Google Search

The buyer searches "NexGen Materials" and lands on the homepage.

3

Quick Scan

They see the supply chain thesis but find no case studies, no downloadable research, no technical depth, and no proof of the lab partnerships.

4

Tab Closed

They leave. No email captured. No way to follow up. The referral momentum cools before a conversation starts.

In a market where your total buyer pool is under 250 people, every lost impression is a lost relationship that may not come back around.
Problem 2

Your Mailing List Captures Interest But Delivers Nothing

You have a working email subscription system. That is more than most early-stage companies in this space. But when someone subscribes, the only thing they receive is a double opt-in confirmation email. After they confirm, they get nothing. No welcome message, no report, no content, no next step.

No Immediate Value Delivered

A new subscriber confirms and hears silence. The moment of highest interest passes without reinforcement. In B2B, the window between "I am curious" and "I moved on" is measured in hours.

No Nurture Sequence

There are no follow-up emails educating the subscriber, sharing your market perspective, or introducing your technology. Every subscriber enters the same void regardless of whether they are a DOE program manager or a procurement VP.

No Segmentation or Next Step

There is no way to distinguish a casual visitor from a high-intent buyer. Everyone gets the same empty experience. A supply chain director at a battery manufacturer and a college student writing a paper are treated identically.

The mailing list infrastructure exists. What is missing is the content and sequence that turns a subscriber into a qualified conversation.
Problem 3

No Content Engine Feeding the Pipeline

nexgenmaterials.com (no /blog, /news, or /resources page exists)

NexGen has proprietary knowledge that no one else in the domestic battery materials space is publishing. Your novel graphite process, your lithium extraction research, your understanding of IRA funding mechanics, your national lab collaboration experience. None of this is being turned into searchable, shareable content.

What Should Be There

Technical briefs on domestic LFP sourcing challenges and NexGen's approach. Market analysis posts on DOE funding rounds, IRA implications, and battery factory buildout timelines. Project narratives around the national lab partnerships that demonstrate credibility. LinkedIn posts linking back to these articles, creating a repeatable content loop.

The companies winning government contracts and JV partnerships are the ones publishing the content that program managers reference in their internal evaluations.
